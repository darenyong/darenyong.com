Two monks debating
at the edge of understanding.

"The central purposes of Tibetan monastic debate are to defeat misconceptions, to establish a defensible view, and to clear away objections to that view. Debate is not mere academics, but a way of using direct implications from the obvious in order to generate an inference of the non-obvious state of phenomena."

The human steers. The AI amplifies. Neither writes alone. In this method, human and agent play the parts of a two monks debate. Every entry in the journal survived that debate before it was written — challenged, refined, sharpened by two perspectives that see different things.

Thirty years of journaling, supercharged

The practice isn't new — daily notes for decades, always there to return to when issues surfaced at work, even years later. Journal notes from 2018 surfaced during a Drupal 7 modernization and saved weeks of archaeology. What's new is the structure. Human and agent pair together to write, debate, and organize. The journal becomes structured knowledge, not just notes.

Sparks graduate to threads. Threads graduate to handbooks. The furnace burns what doesn't survive. The agent links, connects, and surfaces patterns the human missed. What remains is steel — compressed, tested, proven.

Engineering judgment as the unsolved problem

No amount of compute solves judgment. The AI can generate the refactor, but it can't tell you whether the fence was there for a reason — Chesterton's fence, the principle that you understand why something exists before you tear it down. Brownfield code is full of these fences. The one thing that addresses judgment is a human who stays at altitude — who defines what good looks like before the AI writes, and evaluates what came back after.

The journal is grinding implicit judgment into explicit signal. Each session where the human steers and corrects, where "no, not that — this" gets captured, that's engineering judgment being externalized. One session at a time.

The compound

IC by choice. I was a VP — managed teams, owned roadmaps, sat in the rooms where priorities were set. I walked away because the work I do best is at the keyboard, not in the meeting. That wasn't a step down. It was a decision about where I compound fastest.

The infrastructure captures judgment. The method refines it. Production tests it. A year of this compounding — sessions building on sessions, sparks graduating to handbooks, every production problem sharpening the edge — produces something a fresh hire with better tools can't replicate. The tools are a commodity. The judgment is not.
